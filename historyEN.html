Origins & evolution

The History of
Skateboarding

Skateboarding wasn't invented by one person in one day. It's original appearance is out of surf culture, boredom, concrete, and human urge to go fast and look cool doing it.

Key figures

The Godfather

Larry Stevenson

Publisher of Surf Guide magazine, Stevenson was among the first to commercially manufacture skateboards in the early 1960s. In 1969 he patented the kicktail — the upward curve at the back of the deck that made nearly every modern trick possible.


The Inventor

Alan "Ollie" Gelfand

In 1978, a Florida teenager named Alan Gelfand invented the ollie — the foundational trick where the board leaves the ground without the rider touching it. His nickname became the name of the trick. Without the ollie, there is no modern skateboarding.


Timeline

1950s

Surfing Moves to the Streets

California surfers, bored between waves, nailed roller skate wheels to wooden planks. They called it "sidewalk surfing." These crude boards had no kicktail, no grip tape, and no way to turn well — but the feeling was there.

1963

First Commercial Skateboards

Makaha and Hobie begin mass-producing skateboards. The first national skateboarding championship is held in Hermosa Beach, California. Skateboarding briefly becomes a mainstream craze — then dies out as parents call it "too dangerous."

1970s

Urethane Wheels Change Everything

Frank Nasworthy introduces urethane wheels in 1972. Grip and speed improve dramatically. The Z-Boys — a team from Zephyr Surf Shop in Venice, CA — revolutionize skating with an aggressive, low, surfer-influenced style. They skate empty swimming pools during a California drought and invent vertical skating.

1978

The Ollie is Born

Alan Gelfand invents the ollie at a skatepark in Florida. Rodney Mullen later adapts it for flat ground in 1982, and the entire vocabulary of modern street skating follows. The ollie is arguably the single most important moment in skateboarding history.

1980s

Vert Skating & Bones Brigade

Tony Hawk, Steve Caballero, and Rodney Mullen rise to fame through Powell Peralta's Bones Brigade. Vert skating — on massive halfpipes — dominates the era. Skate videos begin to replace magazines as the primary way tricks spread.

1990s

Street Skating Takes Over

The skatepark era fades. Street skating — using curbs, rails, stairs, and public spaces — becomes the dominant style. Mark Gonzales, Natas Kaupas, and Mike Carroll define the era. Skate culture fuses with hip-hop and punk.

1999

Tony Hawk's Pro Skater

The video game releases and introduces skateboarding to millions who had never held a board. Tony Hawk lands the first documented 900 at the X Games the same year. Skating enters mainstream culture permanently.

2021

Olympic Sport

Skateboarding debuts at the Tokyo 2020 Olympics. Yuto Horigome (Japan) wins the first ever men's street gold medal. A sport born from rebellion against authority is now an Olympic discipline — a fact that still divides skaters today.
