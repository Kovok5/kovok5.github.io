From zero to advanced

Tricks

Every trick listed here is ordered from easiest to hardest. Don't skip steps — the basics aren't just a starting point, they're the foundation everything else is built on.

Beginner

Invented by Alan "Ollie" Gelfand, 1978

Ollie

The foundation of nearly every other trick. Pop the tail into the ground, slide your front foot up the board, and level it out in the air. The board leaves the ground without your hands touching it. Nothing else is possible without this.

Manual

Balance on just your back wheels while rolling — skateboarding's version of a wheelie. Great for linking tricks together and essential for street skating on ledges and manual pads.

No single inventor — early vert skating

Drop In

Starting at the top of a ramp by placing your tail on the coping and dropping your weight forward to roll in. A mental trick more than a physical one — looks scary, becomes easy fast.

Intermediate

Invented by Rodney Mullen, 1983

Kickflip

An ollie where you flick your front foot off the nose of the board, causing it to spin lengthwise beneath you. Catch it with your feet and land. Mullen invented this on flat ground — it became the defining trick of street skating.

3 / 5

Invented by Rodney Mullen, 1983

Heelflip

A kickflip in reverse — flick the board with your heel instead of your toe, making it spin the opposite direction. Also a Mullen original. Slightly harder to learn for most people because the motion feels less natural.

3 / 5

Developed in early street skating, 1980s

50-50 Grind

Both trucks lock onto a rail or ledge and you grind forward. One of the first grinds any street skater learns. The challenge is the ollie onto the obstacle — the grind itself is simple once you're locked in.

Advanced

Invented by Rodney Mullen, 1982

360 Flip (Tre Flip)

The board does a kickflip and a 360° backside pop shove-it simultaneously. One of the most celebrated street tricks. Landing your first tre flip is a genuine milestone — most skaters remember the exact moment.

4 / 5

Popularized on vert in the 1980s

900

Two and a half full rotations (900°) in the air on a halfpipe, the hardest trick so far. Tony Hawk became the first person to land it in competition at the 1999 X Games after over ten years of trying. It remains one of the most iconic moments in sports history.
